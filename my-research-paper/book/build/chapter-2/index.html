Chapter 2: Foundations of Robotics

Building upon the introduction to Physical AI and Humanoid Robotics, this chapter delves into the fundamental principles that underpin the field of robotics. Understanding these foundations is crucial for appreciating the complexities and capabilities of modern robots, especially those designed to mimic human characteristics.

Defining a Robot​

At its core, a robot is an autonomous or semi-autonomous machine designed to perform specific tasks. Key characteristics often include:

  • Perception: The ability to sense its environment using various sensors (e.g., cameras, lidar, microphones, tactile sensors).
  • Processing/Intelligence: The capacity to interpret sensory data, make decisions, and plan actions. This is where AI plays a significant role.
  • Actuation: The ability to perform physical actions using motors, hydraulics, pneumatics, and other mechanisms.
  • Manipulation: The capability to interact with objects in the environment, often involving grippers, manipulators, or entire robotic arms.
  • Mobility: The means by which a robot can move within its environment, ranging from wheeled bases to legs for bipedal or quadrupedal locomotion.

Key Components of a Robotic System​

Every robotic system, regardless of its complexity, comprises several essential components working in concert:

1. Mechanical Structure​

This includes the physical body, linkages, joints, and end-effectors of the robot. For humanoid robots, this structure is often designed to resemble the human skeleton and musculature, providing a framework for movement and interaction. Materials science plays a critical role in developing lightweight, strong, and flexible structures.

2. Sensors​

Sensors are the robot's eyes, ears, and touch. They provide the robot with data about its internal state and external environment.

  • Proprioceptive Sensors: Measure the robot's internal state (e.g., joint angles, motor speeds, force/torque sensors).
  • Exteroceptive Sensors: Gather information about the external environment (e.g., cameras for vision, lidar/radar for ranging, microphones for sound, pressure sensors for touch).

3. Actuators​

Actuators are the muscles of the robot, responsible for generating movement. Common types include:

  • Electric Motors: Stepper motors, servo motors.
  • Hydraulic/Pneumatic Actuators: Used for high force applications.
  • Smart Materials: Emerging technologies like shape memory alloys and electroactive polymers.

4. Controller​

The controller is the "brain" that processes sensor data, executes algorithms, and sends commands to the actuators. It can range from simple microcontrollers for basic tasks to powerful onboard computers running complex AI algorithms for advanced humanoid robots.

5. Power Source​

Robots require energy to operate. This can come from batteries (for mobile robots), tethered power supplies, or even advanced energy harvesting techniques.

Types of Robots​

Robots come in many forms, each suited for different applications:

  • Industrial Robots: Typically stationary arms performing repetitive tasks in manufacturing.
  • Mobile Robots: Designed for movement, such as automated guided vehicles (AGVs) or drones.
  • Service Robots: Assist humans in various tasks, from vacuum cleaners to surgical robots.
  • Humanoid Robots: Mimic human appearance and behavior, designed to operate in human environments.

Robotics Disciplines​

The study of robotics is highly interdisciplinary, drawing from:

  • Mechanical Engineering: For design, kinematics, and dynamics.
  • Electrical Engineering: For sensors, actuators, and control electronics.
  • Computer Science/AI: For perception, planning, decision-making, and learning.
  • Materials Science: For advanced robot bodies and components.
  • Biomechanics: Particularly relevant for humanoid and bio-inspired robotics.

This foundational understanding sets the stage for exploring the intelligent aspects of robotics in the subsequent chapters, particularly how AI imbues these physical machines with advanced capabilities.
